## Buyer Profile by Segment

The procurement pain is real across all four segments, but the buyer persona, decision trigger, and purchase process differ materially. Selling to a Solo owner requires a different value prop than selling to a Growth-segment controller.

### Solo Segment (1–4 employees) — $49/mo Solo tier
**Who buys:** The owner, alone. There is no one else. The owner is simultaneously the estimator, field worker, PM, and bookkeeper. They work from a truck or kitchen table.
**What they care about:** Stopping lost receipts from becoming unrecoverable expenses at tax time. They don't think in terms of "three-way match" — they think "I paid $400 at the supply house and I can't remember which job it was for." Job costing for taxes/bids is the primary motivation, not procurement workflow.
**How they make the purchase decision:** Impulse + simple math. They see the QBO App Store listing, the $49/mo price, and the "text your receipts" value prop. If it's cheaper than the margin they're losing (it is), they swipe a credit card. No committee, no demo request, no sales call. Decision in 10 minutes.
**Land-and-expand trigger:** When they hire employee #5, the Solo workflow breaks — now there's a field crew member who shouldn't be texting the owner's personal phone. That's the natural Starter upgrade moment at $0 additional CAC.

### Small Segment (5–9 employees) — $99/mo Starter tier
**Who buys:** The owner. They are still the decision-maker for every software purchase. They've recently come off the tools (or are trying to) and are doing admin on evenings and weekends.
**What they care about:** "My guys text me orders and I have to create the PO manually — it takes 20 minutes per order and I do 5+ a day." Time savings is the primary pitch. Secondary: catching supplier errors before paying. They've probably been burned by a double-billed delivery at least once.
**How they make the purchase decision:** Problem-aware, solution-seeking. They search for "construction purchase order QuickBooks" or find Conduit through Reddit/podcasts. They want a free trial with zero friction — no demo, no sales call, no onboarding call. Self-serve signup with results visible in the first hour is the threshold. Annual prepay with 2 months free is compelling because it signals confidence.
**Champion:** Owner only. No internal champion needed.

### Mid Segment (10–19 employees) — Starter→Pro transition (~$139 blended ARPU)
**Who buys:** Owner or emerging office manager/bookkeeper. The bookkeeper is often the internal champion who discovers Conduit while manually reconciling packing slips. They bring it to the owner with a "look how much time this saves" argument.
**What they care about:** The bookkeeper cares about reducing the reconciliation burden — currently spending 15+ hrs/week matching invoices. The owner cares about catching the overcharges they know are happening but can't prove. Both care about "getting off spreadsheets."
**How they make the purchase decision:** The bookkeeper discovers the tool (QBO App Store, ProAdvisor recommendation, or peer recommendation). They do a free trial independently, validate it works with their QBO setup, then present to the owner as a cost/time savings case. Owner approves because the bookkeeper already validated it. Two-person consensus process.
**Key insight:** The QBO ProAdvisor channel is especially valuable here — the bookkeeper's CPA or QBO consultant is often the trusted voice that introduces Conduit. Proactively targeting ProAdvisors pays off at this segment.

### Growth Segment (20–49 employees) — $299/mo Pro tier
**Who buys:** Office Manager, Controller, or Operations Manager. The owner is now strategic; day-to-day procurement issues land on the controller's desk. In some firms the PM advocates for it because they're drowning in invoice exceptions.
**What they care about:** Full 3-way match automation, price variance detection, multi-crew visibility, job costing accuracy for WIP reports. They want audit trail and exception workflows — not just receipt capture. ROI framing: $90,000+/year in savings at $2,990/year subscription. The 30:1 ROI closes this sale.
**How they make the purchase decision:** Longer cycle than Small/Mid — 2-4 week evaluation period, demo likely requested, reference checks possible. Multiple stakeholders (controller + PM + owner). The controller often has veto power. Annual contract preferred (they want stability). ProAdvisor referral or direct outreach via construction industry events/newsletters.
**Retention driver:** Multiple office seats, deep QBO integration, company-specific terminology dictionary — all increase switching cost. The controller who built the job cost code mapping won't want to rebuild it elsewhere. NRR target: 120%+ via seat expansion.

**Trade contractors** (also known as subcontractors or specialty contractors) **typically bring and supply their own materials** for their specific scope of work, though this isn't a hard-and-fast rule—it's determined by the contract terms between the general contractor (GC) and the trade contractor.

### Most Common Practice
- **Trade contractors provide materials** — This is the standard in the majority of construction projects (especially commercial, larger residential, and most subcontracted work).  
  They include the cost of materials in their bid/quote to the GC, purchase them (often at trade/pricing discounts through their accounts), deliver them to the site, and are responsible for them until installed.
- Why this is preferred:
  - Ensures quality, compatibility, and timely delivery (the trade contractor knows exactly what they need for their trade).
  - The trade contractor assumes risk for defects, shortages, or damage to those materials.
  - GCs generally avoid buying materials for subs because it adds risk/liability (e.g., if wrong items arrive or they're damaged, the GC could be blamed).
  - Helps maintain the independent contractor relationship (if the GC supplies all materials + labor, it risks reclassifying the trade contractor as an employee in some jurisdictions).

### When Materials Come from the Site / GC
- **GC-supplied or "owner-furnished" materials** — This happens in specific cases, often called "GC-provided," "builder-supplied," or "material furnished by others" in the contract.
  - Common examples:
    - Bulk/generic items like drywall sheets, certain lumber/framing (especially in residential where the GC buys large quantities).
    - Specialty/finish items the owner or GC wants to control (e.g., high-end fixtures, specific tile, lighting, or appliances selected by the homeowner).
    - Roofing shingles, certain siding, or windows in some builder models.
  - In these cases, the trade contractor uses materials already on site (delivered/stored by the GC or owner), and their bid is for **labor + installation only** (often lower price).
  - Trades like painters, drywallers, or roofers sometimes work this way, especially with smaller or repeat subs.

### Key Factors That Decide
| Factor                  | Trade Contractor Supplies Materials | GC/Site Supplies Materials          |
|-------------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Contract Language**   | "Labor and materials" or "turnkey" bid | "Labor only" or "materials by GC/owner" |
| **Project Type**        | Most commercial/large jobs          | Some residential, custom, or builder-grade |
| **Material Type**       | Trade-specific (wiring, pipe, fixtures) | Bulk/generic or owner-selected finishes |
| **Cost/Risk**           | Trade gets markup/profit on materials; handles risk | GC controls cost/selection; assumes risk |
| **Relationship**        | Standard sub-GC independence        | More common with long-term/repeat subs |

In short: **Expect trade contractors to bring their own materials** in most situations—it's the default and safest/most professional approach. But always check the specific subcontract agreement or bid documents, as exceptions are common depending on negotiation, project size, trade, and region (e.g., practices can vary slightly in places like North Carolina). If you're the owner or GC, clearly spelling this out upfront avoids disputes!
