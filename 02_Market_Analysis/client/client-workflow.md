Specialty contractor firms (trade subcontractors like electricians, plumbers, HVAC, roofers, etc.) handle **materials management** and **invoicing** (including three-way matching) in practical, often streamlined ways that balance cost control, cash flow, and project efficiency. Practices vary by firm size, trade, project type (residential service vs. commercial/new construction), and tools used, but here are the most common approaches based on industry standards.

**Segment note on workflow variation:**

| Segment | PM Role | Procurement initiator | Three-Way Match reality |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Solo (1–4 emp)** | None — owner IS the field worker | Owner texts/calls supplier directly | No match possible: no PO (owner knows verbally), packing slip lost; invoice paid blind |
| **Small (5–9 emp)** | Owner (part-time, from truck) | Field tech texts owner → owner calls supplier | PO skipped; packing slip lost in van; bookkeeper (owner evenings) pays blind |
| **Mid (10–19 emp)** | Emerging PM or senior tech | Tech texts PM → PM calls supplier | PO sometimes created; packing slip usually lost; part-time bookkeeper hunts manually |
| **Growth (20–49 emp)** | Dedicated PM(s) | Tech texts PM → PM (should) create PO | PO sometimes created; 3-way match attempted via spreadsheet/QBO; highest dollar leakage |

The **Solo segment is a special case**: because the owner IS the field worker, the "Van-to-Office" cycle doesn't exist in the same form. The failure is simpler — the owner buys materials, loses the receipt, and can't reconcile the invoice against a specific job weeks later. There is no PM to skip the PO; the owner just never creates one because they're doing everything themselves.

### How They Manage Materials
Trade contractors typically supply their own trade-specific materials (as discussed earlier), so they focus on efficient procurement, tracking, and minimizing waste/storage issues. Key methods include:

- **Procurement and Ordering**:
  - **Direct from suppliers/vendors** — They use established accounts with wholesalers (e.g., Ferguson for plumbing, Graybar for electrical, or local supply houses) for trade discounts (often 20–50% off retail). Many place orders via phone/app/online portals or through dedicated procurement tools.
  - **Just-in-time (JIT) or staged delivery** — To avoid tying up cash or cluttering job sites/vans, they order materials close to installation time. For larger jobs, they might stage deliveries in phases.
  - **Requisition process** — Foremen or project leads submit material requests (via apps like Procore, Buildertrend, or simple spreadsheets) based on project plans/specs. For small firms, the owner often handles this.

- **Inventory and Tracking**:
  - **Vehicle/toolbox stock** — Service-oriented trades (plumbing, HVAC, electrical) keep common items (fittings, wire, pipe, filters) stocked in trucks/vans using a **Kanban/two-bin system** (reorder when low bin empties) to prevent run-outs.
  - **Job-site storage** — Minimal on-site; materials delivered directly to the site or stored briefly. Bulk items (if GC-provided) are used from site stockpiles.
  - **Tools/Software** — Many use field apps (e.g., ServiceTitan for HVAC/plumbing, Fieldwire, or Procore) for real-time tracking of usage, photo documentation of deliveries, and automatic reordering alerts. Small firms might rely on Excel or paper logs.

- **Best Practices to Avoid Issues**:
  - Accurate forecasting from bids/plans to prevent shortages/overbuying.
  - Strong supplier relationships for quick delivery/returns.
  - Regular audits to reduce theft/loss/waste.
  - In residential/remodel work, owners sometimes select/pay for finishes (e.g., fixtures), so the trade only handles installation.

Material mismanagement is a top cause of delays, so efficient firms treat it as critical to profitability.

### How They Handle Invoicing and Three-Way Matching
Trade contractors invoice the **general contractor** (or sometimes directly to the owner), often on **progress billing**, **time & materials (T&M)**, or **lump sum** with change orders. Invoicing includes labor, materials (at cost + markup, typically 10–35%), overhead, and profit.

- **Invoicing Process**:
  - **Billing cycles** — Monthly progress draws (e.g., % complete), milestone-based, or T&M for service/quick jobs. Invoices detail:
    - Labor hours (with rates, timesheets).
    - Materials used (quantities, unit costs, receipts attached).
    - Subtotals, taxes, retainage (if applicable).
  - **Tools** — Software like QuickBooks, Sage, Foundation, Knowify, or integrated platforms (Procore, Buildertrend, ServiceTitan) automate invoice creation, attach PDFs of receipts/timesheets, and track A/R.
  - **Documentation** — Always include backups: timesheets, material receipts/invoices, photos of work, lien waivers (if required).

- **Three-Way Matching (Primarily for Their Own Vendors/Suppliers)**:
  Three-way matching is an **accounts payable (AP)** control, not directly for client invoicing. Specialty contractors apply it when **paying their suppliers** to avoid overpaying, catch errors, or prevent fraud—especially important with tight margins.

  **Standard three-way match process** (PO + Receipt + Invoice):
  1. Issue a **purchase order (PO)** to supplier (even verbal/email for small buys; formal PO for larger).
  2. Receive materials → Foreman signs off on **delivery receipt/ packing slip** (confirms quantity/condition on-site).
  3. Supplier sends **invoice**.
  4. AP (often the owner/bookkeeper in small firms) compares all three:
     - Quantities match?
     - Prices/terms match PO?
     - Items received as expected?
  5. If matched → Approve and pay (via ACH/check). Discrepancies → Resolve with supplier before payment.

  - **In small firms** — Often manual (spreadsheets, paper folders, or QuickBooks attachments). Takes time but prevents costly mistakes.
  - **In mid-size/larger firms** — Automated via AP software (e.g., integrated with Sage Intacct, Stampli, or procurement tools) that flags mismatches, routes approvals (e.g., to PM), and links to accounting.
  - **Why it's crucial** — Construction has high error risk (wrong quantities, double-billing, damaged goods). Skipping it can erode profits quickly.

For **their outgoing invoices to GCs/owners**, they don't do three-way matching (that's the payer's responsibility). Instead, they provide strong substantiation (receipts, logs) to speed approvals and reduce disputes.

In summary, average specialty firms keep materials lean and mobile (JIT + van stock), use digital tools for tracking/invoicing where possible, and apply three-way matching rigorously on the buy side to protect cash flow. In busy markets like Raleigh, NC, efficient material and AP processes are key differentiators—many invest in software as they grow to handle multiple jobs without chaos. If you're running one or dealing with subs, clear contracts on material responsibility and prompt documentation make everything smoother!
