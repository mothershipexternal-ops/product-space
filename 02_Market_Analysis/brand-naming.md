# Brand Naming Analysis: App Name Selection

**Prepared by:** Marketing Specialist (20+ years SaaS naming experience)
**Date:** 2026-03-06
**Status:** DECISION — Primary name selected with full rationale

---

## Executive Summary

After a full read of the research library — problem space, market analysis, competitive landscape, solution architecture, and landing page copy — a clear naming direction emerged. The product occupies a rare strategic position: it is invisible to the user who creates the work (the field tech) and indispensable to the user who processes it (the office). The name must carry the weight of that duality without over-explaining it.

**Recommended Name: Conduit**

**Tagline:** *The channel between field and office.*

---

## Naming Criteria Applied

SaaS names that survive and scale share six properties:

| Criterion | Weight | Why It Matters Here |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Industry resonance** | High | Primary buyers are electricians. A name they recognize from daily work cuts through skepticism. |
| **Metaphoric precision** | High | The name must *show* what the product does, not just label it. |
| **Phonetic memorability** | High | Contractors discuss tools verbally on job sites and podcasts. Easy to say = free marketing. |
| **Domain viability** | Medium | `.com` preferred, `.io` or `get[name].com` acceptable for a pre-Series A product. |
| **Competitive differentiation** | Medium | Must not sound like Kojo, Field Materials, or Remarcable — all three have a "tech-lite" feel. |
| **Scalability** | Low | The name should not lock the product into one trade or one workflow as the product expands. |

## The Shortlist: Five Names, Full Analysis

### 1. Splice

**What it means in the field:**
A splice is the connection point where two electrical wires are joined — a physical act performed by every electrician on every job, every day. When a tech "splices" wires, they are completing a circuit. When Splice (the product) runs, it is completing the procurement circuit: the text message, the packing slip, and the invoice are joined into a verified match.

**Why this name wins:**

- **Single syllable.** No other major competitor name is one syllable. "Kojo" is two. "Field Materials" is a phrase. "Remarcable" is three. One-syllable SaaS names carry disproportionate recall weight — see Slack, Stripe, Drift, Clerk, Loom. Splice fits this tier.
- **Verb and noun.** "Just splice it" functions as both a product command and a brand imperative. Competitors' names are all nouns.
- **Electrician-native.** The primary beachhead market is electrical contractors (77,550 firms, 88% under 20 employees). "Splice" is a word they say before lunch. This is not a coincidence — it is the product name that requires the least cultural translation for the person buying it.
- **Metaphorically exact.** The Three-Way Match literally splices three documents — the PO, the packing slip, and the invoice — into one verified record. The product is a splice in the data circuit between field and office.
- **Differentiated from competitors.** Kojo sounds like a proper noun brand. Field Materials is descriptive and forgettable. Remarcable sounds enterprise. Splice sounds like a tool used by a person who works with their hands. It respects the buyer's identity.
- **Brand story is already written.** "You splice wires to close a circuit. We splice your orders to close your books." This can be the first line of every sales pitch.

**Domain strategy:**
- `getsplice.com` — clean, industry-standard format for pre-brand-equity stage
- `splice.io` — strongly preferred if available; .io carries credibility in construction tech
- `spliceapp.com` — fallback

**Risk and mitigation:**
- Splice.com is owned by a consumer video editing app (unrelated vertical; zero overlap with the B2B construction buyer). This is not a material risk. Stripe did not let the word "stripe" being used by fabric retailers stop them. Domain parking is not trademark coverage.
- USPTO trademark search should be performed before launch (standard practice for any name). The video editing product is in Class 9 (software); a procurement SaaS registration would be in Class 35 (business management software) — different classes, different registrations.

**Best use:** If Conduit has an unresolvable trademark conflict, Splice is the immediate fallback. It is the second-best name in the set.

### 2. Conduit (RECOMMENDED)

**What it means in the field:**
Electrical conduit is the rigid or flexible tube that protects and routes wires through walls, ceilings, and underground. Every electrician installs conduit. It is the physical channel — it does not do the work itself; it enables the work to flow safely from point A to point B. This is a perfect metaphor for the product.

**Why this name wins:**

- **Metaphorically exact.** The product IS the conduit — the invisible channel between field communication and accounting records. Conduit does not describe a feature; it describes what the product *is* in the data circuit between van and office. This is the rarest quality in a SaaS name.
- **Electrician-native.** Every electrician installs conduit every day. The name requires zero translation for the primary buyer. It names the infrastructure layer, not a single action — a better metaphor for a product that runs continuously in the background.
- **Three syllables give it gravitas.** It sounds professional and infrastructure-grade, which matters for an AP controls product being sold to owners who sign checks. "Trustworthy utility" beats "clever tool" in financial software.
- **Differentiated from competitors.** Kojo and Remarcable sound like startup brands. Conduit sounds like something that has been running reliably for decades. For a product that touches financial records, trust wins over wit.
- **Brand story is already written.** "Conduit is the pipe between your field and your books. Your guys text an order in. A PO comes out." This can be the first line of every sales pitch.

**Domain strategy:**
- `getconduit.com` — clean, industry-standard format for pre-brand-equity stage
- `conduit.io` — check availability; the Conduit.io brand operates in data integration, not construction
- `conduitapp.com` — fallback

**Risk and mitigation:**
- Conduit.io exists in the software integration space. USPTO trademark search should be performed before launch. Registration target is Class 35 (business management software); verify differentiation from any existing Class 35 filings.
- Three syllables is a real cost in verbal referral contexts. Mitigation: the field metaphor ("like the pipe?") gives buyers an immediate hook that turns the name into a natural conversation. The bar test exchange is one beat longer than Splice, but it lands with more resonance — buyers who work with conduit daily will remember it.

### 3. Junction

**What it means in the field:**
A junction box is the enclosure where electrical wires are connected and protected — the literal point where circuits meet. In the product context, Junction is where field orders meet office accounting.

**Strengths:**
- Two syllables, professional, construction-native.
- "Junction — where field meets books" is a clean tagline.
- Works well with `junctionhq.com` or `junction.app`.

**Weaknesses:**
- Less emotionally charged than Splice. It describes a place, not an action.
- "Junction" has a geographic connotation (train junction, crossroads) that competes with the electrical meaning. Brand clarity is diluted.
- No verb form. You cannot "junction" something.

---

### 4. Rivet

**What it means in the field:**
A rivet is a fastener that permanently joins two or more pieces of metal. Commonly used in steel construction, structural fabrication, and HVAC ductwork. Riveting something = making it permanent and irreversible.

**Strengths:**
- One syllable.
- Construction metaphor for permanently fastening things together.
- "Rivet your procurement" works as a tagline.
- Secondary meaning: "riveting" = compelling, engaging. Adds brand personality.

**Weaknesses:**
- Rivet.com is owned by a children's reading app (Rivet by Google). While not a direct conflict, it creates confusion in paid search and social.
- "Riveting" as a secondary meaning can feel like a stretch — it pulls the brand toward "interesting content" rather than "financial control."
- Rivet is less resonant with electricians specifically (more of a structural steel/HVAC term). Given the beachhead market is electrical contractors, this is a meaningful gap.

---

### 5. Claro

**What it means:**
Spanish, Italian, and Portuguese for "clear." Evokes clarity, transparency, and light — the promise that chaos becomes visible and manageable.

**Strengths:**
- Five letters, two syllables, easy to say globally.
- Strong emotional resonance: the product brings clarity to a chaotic procurement process.
- Modern, clean aesthetic — fits the SaaS visual language.
- No construction-specific baggage; has room to expand into adjacent markets.

**Weaknesses:**
- Claro is the largest telecom provider in Latin America (35+ countries, 300M+ subscribers). While not a U.S.-focused construction tool, this is a genuine trademark concern for any company with ambitions beyond U.S. borders.
- It is not trade-native. An electrician hearing "Claro" does not immediately connect it to procurement. The metaphor requires more explanation.
- "Clarity" is a positioning claim, not a product feature. It is a promise the landing page has to make; the name does not make it automatically.

## Rejected Directions

| Name | Reason Rejected |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Truss** | Structural engineering term; no verb form; domain likely taken by architecture/structural SaaS |
| **Weld** | Aggressive connotation; more fabrication than electrical; "weld your invoices" is awkward |
| **Latch** | Passive action (latching = waiting); implies the product sits and waits vs. actively matches |
| **VanTrace** | Descriptive of the problem (good for SEO keywords, bad for a brand); two words feels unfinished |
| **Matchbook** | Two syllables but the "book" suffix anchors it to physical stationery, not software |
| **Triform** | Accurately describes three documents but is jargon; no field worker would say this word |
| **Procuro** | Generic; sounds like a procurement procurement tool with no personality |
| **SnapMatch** | Two words; "snap" dates to consumer apps; no construction resonance |

## Recommended Name in Context

### How it reads on the landing page

**Current hero (placeholder):**
> "Your guys already text their orders. Now QuickBooks gets them too."

**With Conduit branding:**
> **Conduit** — *The channel between field and office.*
>
> Field techs text what they need. Conduit reads it. A purchase order appears in QuickBooks — matched to the right job, with zero data entry.

### How it reads in the footer

> `Conduit | Built for trade contractors who text.`

### How it reads in verbal referral (the "bar test")

> "What do you use for material orders?"
> "We use Conduit. You text a number, it drops a PO in QuickBooks."
> "...Conduit? Like the pipe?"
> "Exactly — it's the pipe between your guys and your books."

Two exchanges, not one, but the field metaphor closes the loop on its own. Buyers who run conduit every day will remember this conversation. This is the bar test. Conduit passes.

### How it reads in the email autoresponder

> **Subject:** You're on the Conduit private beta list
> "Hey — thanks for signing up for Conduit..."

Clean. Professional. Infrastructure-grade.
